            Black Bow of Pharis is a Weapon in Dark Souls 3.



            A black longbow named after a hero of old, known for the unusual stance from which it is fired.

            Has a longer range than standard bows, but successful usage requires a trained, dexterous hand.

            Skill: Pharis Triple-shot
            Swiftly nocks three arrows with finesse after drawing the bow, firing them simultaniously. 



            Notes and Tips:

            • Cannot be Infused or Buffed.
            • Reinforced with Titanite.
            • The "unusual stance" mentioned in the description is that the bow is held horizontal when drawn. In comparison the Longbow is held vertical, and this also makes it easy to distinguish the two longbows from eachother in a pvp situation since they look very similar.
            • The bow is a weapon from the arsenal of Pharis, a character that features in Dark Souls 1.
            • Cannot running attack or roll attack.
            • Has the longest range out of all the regular bows.
            • When using the skill of this weapon alongside Dark Arrows, the three split arrows will bend and converge on the point of which you were aiming, and then spread out again.
            • Damage may vary depending on ammunition(arrows).



            Location/Where to Find

            • Dropped by an Elder Ghru in Farron Keep. There are 3 standing in a circle around a Poison Gem, near the zone wall by the Keep Ruins bonfire. To reach them, travel to Keep Ruins, exit down the ramp, jump off to the left and turn left again, hugging the Keep Ruins island. You will see the group ahead and to the right shortly (watch for the satyr dog you'll attract as you proceed). Another in the group drops Pharis's Hat.
            • Video location (fextralife playthrough video starting at 1h 58m  5s)

                                        The funny thing is, that stance is known to improve draw speed and accuracy, but mainly with short bows. Notice how the arrow is nocked with the palm facing downward—this makes the path of the arrow more predictable since the stave doesn’t block user’s view of the arrow. It’s also a faster draw than threading the arrow between the stave and bowstring to recreate the effect in a vertical stance. Realistically, this would likely reduce the range of the arrow since the bow cannot be drawn as far back, but short bows were hardly used from a distance greater than 20-30 feet. Sorry, archery guy likes his archery builds.
